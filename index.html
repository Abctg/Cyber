★ The position

The purest way to own the part of cybersecurity that grows fastest as AI takes over.

AI can now find security holes faster than any company can fix them. That turns a trickle of problems into a flood — and the flood is what makes Tenable's job mission-critical. The bet is simple: as AI multiplies the number of threats, demand for the company that sorts and fixes them grows for years. This is a revenue-growth story, not a cheap-stock story.

Three pillars of the bull case

01 Quality franchise

The category leader, and it's real

Tenable is the leader at finding security weaknesses across a company's whole estate — and has been for 20+ years. About 95% of its revenue is recurring, it serves ~44,000 customers, and it's profitable and cash-generative. This is an established, sticky business — the kind that does well when demand for its core job rises.

02 The AI tailwind

More threats = more demand

The fear was that AI would make Tenable's scanning obsolete. The reality is the opposite: AI floods every company with far more security holes to deal with, and someone has to sort and fix them. The number of known threats roughly doubled in five years even before AI; AI accelerates that curve. Tenable sells exactly the service that flood creates demand for — so it's a beneficiary of the disruption, not a victim.

03 The growth engine

Customers buying more, not just renewing

Tenable's newer all-in-one platform (Tenable One) is already about a third of revenue and growing in the mid-teens — much faster than the legacy scanning business. As more customers move onto it, the company's overall growth rate should climb. That mix-shift is the engine: it's already visible in the numbers today, not just a promise.

Why it can defend itself — the moat boundary, honestly drawn

The fair objection is that the bigger players (CrowdStrike from inside the network, Microsoft/Wiz from the cloud) are moving into the same sort-and-fix job. Tenable's edge is real but has limits — here's exactly where it holds and where it doesn't.

Where Tenable holds
Where it's contested
  • The messy, non-cloud estate — factory and industrial systems, older on-site infrastructure, and the long tail of devices the cloud-only players simply can't see.
  • Vendor-neutral — it pulls in findings from any tool, not just its own. Big companies that run many vendors prefer a neutral referee.
  • An early-warning edge — it tends to flag threats being actively exploited about a week before the main government list does.
  • The cloud — Microsoft/Wiz is the leader here and is building the same neutral-ingestion feature to chip away at Tenable's independence advantage.
  • Inside the network — CrowdStrike is already on the device and can add the sort-and-fix job almost for free.
  • The pure sorters — several smaller specialists do only the sorting job, proving it can be sold separately from the scanning.

The case is not "Tenable wins the whole market." It's "Tenable owns a defensible, hard-to-copy corner of it — the broad, messy, non-cloud estate plus neutral, any-vendor sorting — and that corner grows as AI multiplies threats."

The product that proves they get it — "Hexa"

In May, Tenable launched Hexa, an AI assistant (powered by Claude) built into its platform. The simple version: instead of just handing a security team a long list of problems, Hexa sorts them by what actually matters, then does much of the fixing automatically — writing the tickets, applying the fixes, generating the reports. That's the leap from "here's what's wrong" to "here's what's wrong and it's handled." It's the clearest sign management understands that the valuable job is now sorting-and-fixing, not just finding. The honest caveat: it's early, and Hexa isn't a big revenue driver yet — it's mostly there to get customers onto the bigger platform. The thing to watch is customers actually paying more for it over time.

3 The catalyst — Mythos & Glasswing

The moment finding stopped being the hard part.

Anthropic's most advanced model class showed AI can discover and chain software vulnerabilities at a scale no human team or legacy scanner matches. Through Project Glasswing, these capabilities surfaced more than 10,000 high- or critical-severity vulnerabilities in real, critical systems.

For two decades, progress was limited by how fast you could find vulnerabilities. Now it's limited by how fast you can verify, prioritise and patch the flood AI uncovers.

This is the catalyst for the Tenable case specifically. If finding problems becomes easy and abundant, the value shifts to sorting and fixing them — and that's exactly what Tenable's platform does. Crucially, Anthropic has said it wants to be a general AI company, not a cybersecurity vendor — so the AI labs increase demand for what Tenable sells rather than competing with it. And Tenable is one of the security companies the labs chose to partner with, which Qualys is not.

What Mythos does for Tenable

It turns a trickle of security problems into a flood — far more for every company to deal with. That flood is exactly what creates demand for Tenable's sort-and-fix service. More problems = more need for the company that handles them.

What Glasswing signals

The labs distribute capability through vendors. Tenable is a partner, not a target — and the named partner, not a bystander. Thesis-consistent validation from the source of the disruption itself.

5 The honest risks — and the rebuttal

Why this could be wrong, and why I'd still take the bet.

The case is strong but not free. Each risk gets a fair statement and the strongest counter — the bet only holds if it survives its own bear case.

A bigger rival takes the job

CrowdStrike (from inside the network) and Microsoft/Wiz (from the cloud) are both moving into the same sorting-and-fixing job — and they can bundle it cheaply with what customers already buy.

The rebuttalNeither fully covers the messy non-cloud parts — factory systems, industrial equipment, older on-site infrastructure — and both want to lock you into one vendor. Tenable's breadth and independence is a real edge, and the flood of AI-found threats grows the whole market, including its slice.
The growth might not show up

The case needs the bigger platform to pull overall growth higher; today growth is ~10% and slowing. Some analysts won't bank on the speed-up until they see it.

The rebuttalThe whole case is that growth arrives as AI multiplies threats — and the recent AI partnerships and product launches are early evidence it's starting to. The signal to track is the bigger platform pulling total growth up, quarter by quarter.
The AI product is early

Hexa, the AI assistant, isn't a meaningful revenue driver yet — right now it's more of a hook to win customers than a money-maker.

The rebuttalThat's fine — the case doesn't depend on AI revenue today. It depends on the threat flood driving more customers onto the platform over the next few years. The AI product getting paid for later is a bonus, not the core bet.
It defended against the wrong threat

Tenable argued the AI labs aren't rivals (they don't sit inside company networks). True — but most breaches don't come from the code flaws AI finds; they come from bad settings and weak accounts, which is more CrowdStrike's territory.

The rebuttalThose weak spots — bad settings and over-permissioned accounts, especially across factory systems and on-site infrastructure — are exactly where Tenable's broad coverage is strongest. It's contested ground, not conceded.
It could just stay slow

If the faster-growing platform doesn't pull overall growth up, you're left with a steady but unexciting ~10% grower — fine, but not the story.

The rebuttalEven in that case it's a profitable, growing business — not a broken one. And the threat flood is a strong reason to expect growth to speed up, not stall.
Concentration & macro

~15% of revenue comes from governments (subject to budget cycles), it leans on resellers to sell, and it carries some debt. A broad tech-stock selloff could drag it regardless of the story.

The rebuttalGovernment security spending is sticky and mission-critical, and the demand driver here — AI multiplying threats — is largely independent of the economic cycle.
The verdict

Tenable is the purest way to own the part of cybersecurity that AI makes busier — the sorting-and-fixing layer — and it works with both leading AI labs to do it.

The bet is about growth, not about the stock getting more expensive. As AI multiplies the number of security threats every company faces, the work of sorting and fixing them grows for years — and that's Tenable's core job. It doesn't have the widest moat in the industry (the giants do), but it owns a real, defensible corner — the broad, messy estate that the cloud-only players can't reach — and that corner expands with the threat flood. The risk is that a bigger rival muscles in on the same job; the opportunity is a focused, profitable business riding one of the clearest demand waves in software. The whole thesis lives or dies on whether that revenue growth actually shows up — so that's the one thing to watch.
